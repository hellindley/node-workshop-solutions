<p><i><b>Tintin in Tibet</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i><b>Tintin au Tibet</b></i></span>) is the twentieth volume of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">The Adventures of Tintin</a></i>, the comics series by Belgian cartoonist <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9" title="Hergé">Hergé</a>. It was serialised weekly from September 1958 to November 1959 in <i><a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">Tintin</a></i> magazine and published as a book in 1960. Hergé considered it his favourite <i>Tintin</i> adventure and an emotional effort, as he created it while suffering from traumatic nightmares and a personal conflict while deciding to leave his wife of three decades for a younger woman. The story tells of the young reporter <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(character)" title="Tintin (character)">Tintin</a> in search of his friend <a href="/wiki/Chang_Chong-Chen" title="Chang Chong-Chen">Chang Chong-Chen</a>, who the authorities claim has died in a plane crash in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>. Convinced that Chang has survived, Tintin leads his companions across the Himalayas to the plateau of <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, along the way encountering the mysterious <a href="/wiki/Yeti" title="Yeti">Yeti</a>.</p>
<p>Following <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_Sea_Sharks" title="The Red Sea Sharks">The Red Sea Sharks</a></i> (1958) and its large number of characters, <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> differs from other stories in the series in that it features only a few familiar characters and is also Hergé's only adventure not to pit Tintin against an antagonist. Themes in Hergé's story include <a href="/wiki/Extrasensory_perception" title="Extrasensory perception">extrasensory perception</a>, the mysticism of <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>, and friendship. <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> has been translated into 32 languages, is highly regarded by critics, and has been praised by the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a>, who awarded it the <a href="/wiki/Light_of_Truth_Award" title="Light of Truth Award">Light of Truth Award</a>. The story was a commercial success and was published in book form by <a href="/wiki/Casterman" title="Casterman">Casterman</a> shortly after its conclusion; the series itself became a defining part of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Belgian_comics" title="Franco-Belgian comics">Franco-Belgian comics tradition</a>. <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> was adapted for <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Tintin (TV series)">television</a>, radio, documentary, theatre, and a <a href="/wiki/Tintin_in_Tibet_(video_game)" title="Tintin in Tibet (video game)">video game</a>, and has been the subject of a museum exhibition.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While on holiday at a resort in the <a href="/wiki/French_Alps" title="French Alps">French Alps</a> with <a href="/wiki/Snowy_(character)" title="Snowy (character)">Snowy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Captain_Haddock" title="Captain Haddock">Captain Haddock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Professor_Calculus" title="Professor Calculus">Professor Calculus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(character)" title="Tintin (character)">Tintin</a> reads about a plane crash in the <a href="/wiki/Gosainthan" title="Gosainthan" class="mw-redirect">Gosain Than</a> Massif in the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> of Tibet. He then has a vision of his friend <a href="/wiki/Chang_Chong-Chen" title="Chang Chong-Chen">Chang Chong-Chen</a>, badly injured and calling for help from the wreckage of the crashed plane. He flies to <a href="/wiki/Kathmandu" title="Kathmandu">Kathmandu</a> with Snowy and a sceptical Captain Haddock. They hire a <a href="/wiki/Sherpa_(people)" title="Sherpa (people)" class="mw-redirect">Sherpa</a> named <a href="/wiki/Tharkey" title="Tharkey" class="mw-redirect">Tharkey</a> and, accompanied by porters, travel overland from Nepal towards the crash site.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A919601.E2.80.9327_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A919601.E2.80.9327-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The porters abandon the group in fear when mysterious <a href="/wiki/Yeti" title="Yeti">Yeti</a> tracks are found, while Tintin, Haddock and Tharkey go on and eventually reach the crash site. Tintin sets off with Snowy to trace Chang's steps, and, after glimpsing what seems to be a human silhouette in a snowstorm, finds a cave where Chang has carved his name on a rock. Tharkey believes that Tintin saw the Yeti and convinces him to abandon his friend and return with him to Nepal, since the area is too large to search. Tintin spots a scarf on a cliff face, concludes Chang is near, and continues with only the Captain. While attempting to climb upwards, Haddock slips and hangs down the cliff wall, imperilling Tintin, who is tied to him. He tells Tintin to cut the rope to save himself, but Tintin refuses. Haddock tries to cut it himself, but drops his knife, alerting Tharkey, who has returned in time to rescue them. They try to camp for the night but lose their tent and must trek onwards, unable to sleep lest they freeze, arriving within sight of the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Buddhist</a> monastery of Khor-Biyong before collapsing from exhaustion.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A9196026.E2.80.9344_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A9196026.E2.80.9344-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Blessed_Lightning" title="Blessed Lightning" class="mw-redirect">Blessed Lightning</a>, a monk at the monastery, has a vision of Tintin, Snowy, Haddock, and Tharkey in peril. Tintin regains consciousness and, unable to help himself, gives Snowy a note to deliver. Snowy runs to the monastery and is recognised as the dog from Blessed Lightning's vision. Tintin, Haddock and Tharkey regain consciousness in the monastery and are brought before the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Abbot" title="Grand Abbot" class="mw-redirect">Grand Abbot</a>. The Abbot tells Tintin to abandon his quest, but Blessed Lightning has another vision, through which Tintin learns that Chang is still alive inside a mountain cave at the Horn of the Yak—and that the Yeti is also there. Tintin and Haddock travel on to the Horn of the Yak.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A9196044.E2.80.9354_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A9196044.E2.80.9354-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>They arrive at a cave. Tintin ventures inside and finds Chang, who is feverish and shaking. The Yeti suddenly appears, revealed as a large <a href="/wiki/Simian" title="Simian">anthropoid</a>, reacting with anger at Tintin's attempt to take Chang away. Tintin sets off the flash bulb of his camera, scaring the Yeti into fleeing. Chang explains that the Yeti saved his life after the crash. The Grand Abbot presents Tintin with a silk scarf in honour of the bravery he has shown for his friend Chang. As the party travels home, Chang muses that the Yeti is not a wild animal, but has a human soul. The Yeti sadly watches their departure from a distance.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A9196054.E2.80.9362_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerg.C3.A9196054.E2.80.9362-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_early_ideas">Background and early ideas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Background and early ideas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Photograph of a snowy, mountainous landscape" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg/220px-Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg/330px-Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg/440px-Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Cwm_-_14th_May_2011.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hergé amassed a collection of clippings and used pictures similar to this image of the Tibetan landscape as inspiration for his mountainscape drawings.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In October 1957, Hergé sent his publisher, <a href="/wiki/Casterman" title="Casterman">Casterman</a>, the cover of his completed nineteenth <i>Tintin</i> adventure, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_Sea_Sharks" title="The Red Sea Sharks">The Red Sea Sharks</a></i>, and for several weeks considered a number of plot ideas for his next story.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201193.E2.80.9394LofficierLofficier200272_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201193.E2.80.9394LofficierLofficier200272-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Fondly recalling the <a href="/wiki/Scouting" title="Scouting">Scouting</a> days of his youth, his first idea was to send Tintin back to the United States, as in the third adventure, <i><a href="/wiki/Tintin_in_America" title="Tintin in America">Tintin in America</a></i>, to help a group of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Natives</a> defend their land from a large corporation that wished to drill for oil. However, Hergé came to believe that retracing old ground would be a step backward.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171Farr2001162Assouline2009187Goddin201196_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171Farr2001162Assouline2009187Goddin201196-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Another idea had Tintin striving to prove that Haddock's butler <a href="/wiki/Nestor_(comics)" title="Nestor (comics)">Nestor</a> was framed for a crime committed by his old employers, the <a href="/wiki/Bird_brothers" title="Bird brothers" class="mw-redirect">Bird brothers</a>. He dismissed this as well,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171LofficierLofficier200272.E2.80.9373Assouline2009187Goddin201194Peeters2012270_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171LofficierLofficier200272.E2.80.9373Assouline2009187Goddin201194Peeters2012270-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> but kept the idea of an adventure with no guns or violence. This was to become the only <i>Tintin</i> story without an antagonist.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171Peeters1989110Assouline2009191Goddin2011101_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171Peeters1989110Assouline2009191Goddin2011101-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup> A third idea sent Tintin and Professor Calculus to a snow-covered polar region, where a stranded group of explorers need Calculus to save them from <a href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning" class="mw-redirect">food poisoning</a>. He abandoned this plot as well, but kept the setting in a snowy environment and decided to focus, not on Calculus, but on his main character Tintin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171.E2.80.93172_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171.E2.80.93172-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A collaborator of Hergé's, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Van_Melkebeke" title="Jacques Van Melkebeke">Jacques Van Melkebeke</a>, had suggested in 1954 to set a story in Tibet, likely influenced by the play he adapted for Hergé in the 1940s, <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>M. Boullock A Disparu</i></span> (<i>Mr. Boullock's Disappearance</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200273.E2.80.9374.2C_91Peeters2012271_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200273.E2.80.9374.2C_91Peeters2012271-15"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Heuvelmans" title="Bernard Heuvelmans">Bernard Heuvelmans</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cryptozoology" title="Cryptozoology">cryptozoologist</a> who had helped Hergé envision lunar exploration for the two-part <i><a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(comics)" title="Destination Moon (comics)">Destination Moon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Explorers_on_the_Moon" title="Explorers on the Moon">Explorers on the Moon</a></i>, had given him a copy of his book <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Sur La Piste Des Bêtes Ignorées</i></span> (<i>On the Trail of Unknown Animals</i>) in 1955,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001165Assouline2009187Peeters2012272LofficierLofficier200274_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001165Assouline2009187Peeters2012272LofficierLofficier200274-16"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> inscribing on the inside the suggestion that one day Tintin should meet the Yeti.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin201196_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin201196-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1958, Hergé decided that Tibet would be the setting of Tintin's next <i>Adventure</i>. Initial ideas for the title were <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Le Museau de la Vache</i></span> (<i>The Cow's Snout</i>), <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Le Museau de l'Ours</i></span> (<i>The Bear's Snout</i>), and <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Le Museau du Yak</i></span> (<i>The Yak's Snout</i>), all of which refer to the mountain in the latter part of the story.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001168Assouline2009191Goddin2011101.E2.80.93103LofficierLofficier200273_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001168Assouline2009191Goddin2011101.E2.80.93103LofficierLofficier200273-17"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Although it was initially claimed that "market research" chose the title <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> suggesting sales would be better if the book used Tintin's name in the title, entertainment producer and author <a href="/wiki/Harry_Thompson" title="Harry Thompson">Harry Thompson</a> suggested "the title reflected the solo nature of [Tintin's] undertaking."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173-18"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Herg.C3.A9.27s_psychological_issues">Hergé's psychological issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hergé's psychological issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hergé reached a particularly traumatic period in his life and suffered a <a href="/wiki/Mental_breakdown" title="Mental breakdown">mental breakdown</a>. In 1956, he realised that he had fallen out of love with his wife <a href="/wiki/Germaine_Kieckens" title="Germaine Kieckens">Germaine</a>, whom he had married in 1932, and by 1958, he and Fanny Vlaminck, a colourist at <a href="/wiki/Studios_Herg%C3%A9" title="Studios Hergé">Studios Hergé</a> twenty-eight years his junior, had developed a deep mutual attraction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991168Peeters1989110Farr2001161LofficierLofficier200215.2C_74Goddin2011101Peeters2012260_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991168Peeters1989110Farr2001161LofficierLofficier200215.2C_74Goddin2011101Peeters2012260-19"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> They began courting; Hergé's new companion lifted his morale and shared many of his interests.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991168Farr2001161_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991168Farr2001161-20"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Germaine soon began interfering with the courtship, causing Hergé to admit his desire had been to maintain a relationship with both women.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012280_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012280-21"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> When he failed to please either, he began to contemplate divorcing Germaine to marry Fanny.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161Assouline2009186Goddin2011109_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161Assouline2009186Goddin2011109-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholic</a> upbringing and <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scout" title="Boy Scout" class="mw-redirect">Boy Scout</a> ethic, however, caused him to feel tremendous guilt.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991168.2C_170Farr2001161_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991168.2C_170Farr2001161-23"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> As he later related to interviewer <a href="/wiki/Numa_Sadoul" title="Numa Sadoul">Numa Sadoul</a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"It meant turning upside down all my values—what a shock! This was a serious moral crisis: I was married, and I loved someone else; life seemed impossible with my wife, but on the other hand I had this scout-like idea of giving my word for ever. It was a real catastrophe. I was completely torn up."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESadoul1975_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESadoul1975-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>During this period, Hergé had recurrent nightmares where he faced images of what he described as "the beauty and cruelty of white"—visions of white and snow that he could not explain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991170Goddin2011104Sadoul1975_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991170Goddin2011104Sadoul1975-25"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> As he later told Sadoul:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"At the time, I was going through a time of real crisis and my dreams were nearly always white dreams. And they were extremely distressing. I took note of them and remember one where I was in a kind of tower made up of a series of ramps. Dead leaves were falling and covering everything. At a particular moment, in an immaculately white alcove, a white skeleton appeared that tried to catch me. And then instantly everything around me became white."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESadoul1975_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESadoul1975-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>At the advice of his former editor <a href="/wiki/Raymond_de_Becker" title="Raymond de Becker">Raymond de Becker</a>, Hergé travelled to Zürich to consult the Swiss <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalyst</a> <a href="/wiki/Franz_Riklin" title="Franz Riklin">Franz Riklin</a>, a student of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a>, to decipher his disturbing dreams.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011108McCarthy200690Assouline2009190.E2.80.93191Peeters2012274.2C_278LofficierLofficier200215.2C_74_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011108McCarthy200690Assouline2009190.E2.80.93191Peeters2012274.2C_278LofficierLofficier200215.2C_74-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Riklin latched on to the "quest for purity" that featured so prominently in Hergé's dreams, and ultimately in <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011108_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011108-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> He told the author that he must destroy "the demon of purity" in his mind as soon as possible:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"I do not want to discourage you, but you will never reach the goal of your work. It comes to one or the other: you must overcome your crisis, or continue your work. But, in your place, I would stop immediately!"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171Farr2001161Assouline2009191Goddin2011108_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171Farr2001161Assouline2009191Goddin2011108-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Although Hergé was tempted to abandon <i>Tintin</i> at Riklin's suggestion, devoting himself instead to his hobby of <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract art</a>, he felt that doing so would be an acceptance of failure.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> In the end, he left his wife to marry Fanny Vlaminck and continued work on <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171.2C_174Farr2001161Assouline2009191Goddin2011109Peeters2012278.E2.80.93279_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171.2C_174Farr2001161Assouline2009191Goddin2011109Peeters2012278.E2.80.93279-30"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> trusting that completing the book would exorcise the demons he felt possessed him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172Peeters1989110Goddin2011108Assouline2009191_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172Peeters1989110Goddin2011108Assouline2009191-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> "It was a brave decision, and a good one", said reporter and British Tintin expert <a href="/wiki/Michael_Farr" title="Michael Farr">Michael Farr</a>. "Few problems, psychological included, are solved by abandoning them."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Thompson noted, "It was ironic, but not perhaps unpredictable, that faced with the moral dilemma posed by Riklin, Hergé chose to keep his Scout's word of honour to Tintin, but not to Germaine."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>c<span>]</span></a></sup> Belgian Tintin expert <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Goddin" title="Philippe Goddin">Philippe Goddin</a> summarized: "[Hergé] sought to regain a lost equilibrium, that he imposes on his hero a desire to seek purity&#160;... considering it necessary for Tintin to go through the intimate experience of distress and loneliness&#160;... and discover himself."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011104.2C_107_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011104.2C_107-35"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In creating <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>, Hergé drew upon a range of influences. Setting it in the Himalayas, a snow-covered environment, followed his recurring dreams of whiteness and his need to create an adventure that "must be a solo voyage of redemption" from the "whiteness of guilt".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172-36"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> The idea of a solo voyage led to Tintin being accompanied only by Snowy, their guide, and a reluctant Haddock—who supplies the needed counterpoint and humour.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172Peeters1989110Farr2001161_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172Peeters1989110Farr2001161-37"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Drigung_monastery10.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Photograph of a red Asian temple on a mountainside" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Drigung_monastery10.jpg/220px-Drigung_monastery10.jpg" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Drigung_monastery10.jpg/330px-Drigung_monastery10.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Drigung_monastery10.jpg/440px-Drigung_monastery10.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3780" data-file-height="2189" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Drigung_monastery10.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Drigung_Monastery" title="Drigung Monastery">Drigung Monastery</a> in the Himalayas of Tibet, similar to the Buddhist monastery depicted in the book</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>While considering the character of Chang, absent since <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Lotus" title="The Blue Lotus">The Blue Lotus</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Hergé thought of his artistic Chinese friend <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Chongren" title="Zhang Chongren">Zhang Chongren</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200647.E2.80.9348_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy200647.E2.80.9348-38"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> whom he had not seen since the days of their friendship over twenty years earlier. Hergé and Zhang used to spend every Sunday together, during which Hergé learned much about Chinese culture for his work on <i>The Blue Lotus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters201274.E2.80.9376_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters201274.E2.80.9376-39"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>d<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, Zhang moved back to his homeland and Hergé lost contact with his friend after the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Japanese invasion of China</a> in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELopez.2C_Jr.1999212Thompson1991172Goddin2011101Peeters2012318.E2.80.93321_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELopez.2C_Jr.1999212Thompson1991172Goddin2011101Peeters2012318.E2.80.93321-41"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Hergé felt Chang and Tintin must be reunited, just as he hoped to see his friend again someday.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172Goddin2011101LofficierLofficier200273_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172Goddin2011101LofficierLofficier200273-42"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>e<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hergé read a variety of books about Tibet for this project: <a href="/wiki/Fosco_Maraini" title="Fosco Maraini">Fosco Maraini</a>'s <i>Secret Tibet</i>, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Harrer" title="Heinrich Harrer">Heinrich Harrer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Years_in_Tibet" title="Seven Years in Tibet">Seven Years in Tibet</a></i>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tsewang_Pemba&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tsewang Pemba (page does not exist)">Tsewang Pemba</a>'s <i>Tibet my Homeland</i>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Herzog" title="Maurice Herzog">Maurice Herzog</a>'s <i>Annapurna</i>, discredited author <a href="/wiki/Lobsang_Rampa" title="Lobsang Rampa">Lobsang Rampa</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Third_Eye_(book)" title="The Third Eye (book)">The Third Eye</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>f<span>]</span></a></sup> and the books of Belgian <a href="/wiki/Exploration" title="Exploration">explorer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spiritualism" title="Spiritualism">spiritualist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexandra_David-N%C3%A9el" title="Alexandra David-Néel">Alexandra David-Néel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009185.E2.80.93186Peeters2012273LofficierLofficier200274.E2.80.9375_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009185.E2.80.93186Peeters2012273LofficierLofficier200274.E2.80.9375-47"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Hergé visited the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Belgian_Alpine_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Belgian Alpine Society (page does not exist)">Belgian Alpine Society</a> to examine their photographic collection of the Himalayas, and they sent him photographer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Lannoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Richard Lannoy (page does not exist)">Richard Lannoy</a>'s work on India.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009188_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009188-48"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Models for drawings such as of monks with musical instruments, Sherpas with backpacks, and the airplane wreckage came from clippings Hergé had amassed from sources such as <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001166.E2.80.93168_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001166.E2.80.93168-49"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Members of the Studios helped him gather other source material; for instance, collaborator <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Martin_(comics)" title="Jacques Martin (comics)">Jacques Martin</a> researched and drew the story's costumes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172.E2.80.93173_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991172.E2.80.93173-50"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To learn about the Yeti, which he depicted as a benevolent creature, Hergé contacted his friend Bernard Heuvelmans, the author of <i>On the Trail of Unknown Animals</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001165Assouline2009187Peeters2012272LofficierLofficier200274_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001165Assouline2009187Peeters2012272LofficierLofficier200274-16"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> After re-reading Heuvelmans' description of the Yeti, Hergé went on to research the <a href="/wiki/Cryptid" title="Cryptid">cryptid</a> species as much as possible.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters1989112Farr2001165Peeters2012271LofficierLofficier200274Goddin201196_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters1989112Farr2001165Peeters2012271LofficierLofficier200274Goddin201196-51"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Hergé interviewed mountaineers, including Herzog, who had spotted the tracks of what he believed was an enormous biped that stopped at the foot of a rock face on <a href="/wiki/Annapurna" title="Annapurna">Annapurna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001165Peeters2012272.E2.80.93273_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001165Peeters2012272.E2.80.93273-52"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Even the creature's care for the starving Chang derives from a Sherpa account of a Yeti that rescued a little girl in similar circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001165_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Farr2001165-53"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Another influence came from Fanny Vlaminck, who was interested in <a href="/wiki/Extrasensory_perception" title="Extrasensory perception">extrasensory perception</a> and the mysticism of Tibetan Buddhism, prominent themes in the story,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162-54"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> which also fascinated Hergé.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162Thompson1991174Peeters1989112LofficierLofficier200274_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162Thompson1991174Peeters1989112LofficierLofficier200274-55"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">Publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plane_crash_in_Tintin_(300x169).jpg" class="image"><img alt="A comic-strip panel of an airplane crashed in a mountainous area, covered in snow" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/df/Plane_crash_in_Tintin_%28300x169%29.jpg" width="300" height="169" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="169" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plane_crash_in_Tintin_(300x169).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Panel from <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>, depicting the plane wreckage. When <a href="/wiki/Air_India" title="Air India">Air India</a> reacted negatively to having their plane pictured in a crash, Hergé changed the logo to the fictional Sari-Airways.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Studios Hergé serialised <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> weekly from September 1958 to November 1959, two pages per week, in <i><a href="/wiki/Tintin_(magazine)" title="Tintin (magazine)">Tintin</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200272Thompson1991130_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200272Thompson1991130-56"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to his desire for accuracy, Hergé added the logo of <a href="/wiki/Air_India" title="Air India">Air India</a> to the airplane crash debris. A representative of Air India complained to Hergé about the adverse publicity the airline might suffer, arguing, "It's scandalous, none of our aircraft has ever crashed; you have done us a considerable wrong." Air India had cooperated with Hergé, facilitating his research by providing him reading material, contemporary photographs, and film footage of India and Nepal, particularly Delhi and Kathmandu.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001168Peeters1989112Goddin2011103_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001168Peeters1989112Goddin2011103-57"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>g<span>]</span></a></sup> While the crashed aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_registration" title="Aircraft registration">tail number</a> remained "VT", the country code for all Indian aircraft, Hergé agreed to change the airline logo in the published edition to the fictional Sari-Airways, dryly noting that there were so many Indian airlines it was possible that there really was a Sari-Airways.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001168Peeters1989112_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001168Peeters1989112-60"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While developing the story, members of the Studios confronted Hergé with concerns about elements of <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>. <a href="/wiki/Bob_de_Moor" title="Bob de Moor">Bob de Moor</a> feared the scene in which Haddock crashes into a <a href="/wiki/Stupa" title="Stupa">stupa</a> was disrespectful to Buddhists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012279_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012279-61"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Jacques van Melkebeke suggested that the Yeti not be depicted to create a sense of enigma; Hergé disagreed, instinctively believing that it would disappoint his child readers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012279_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012279-61"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After the serial concluded, Hergé worked with his publisher, Casterman, to produce the work in book form. Hergé's original design for the front cover featured Tintin and his expedition standing on a backdrop of pure white.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011116_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoddin2011116-62"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Casterman deemed it too abstract, so Hergé added a mountain range at the top; biographer <a href="/wiki/Beno%C3%AEt_Peeters" title="Benoît Peeters">Benoît Peeters</a> expressed that in doing so, the image was deprived of some of its "strength and originality".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012279_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012279-61"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During production, Hergé kept abreast of the turbulent political developments in Tibet.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162Goddin2011107_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162Goddin2011107-63"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> In March 1959, Tibet's foremost political and spiritual leader, the <a href="/wiki/14th_Dalai_Lama" title="14th Dalai Lama">Dalai Lama</a>, fled the region into self-imposed exile in India following disagreements with China's governing <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162French2009Le_Soir_23_May2001_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001162French2009Le_Soir_23_May2001-64"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> In May 2001, when <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> was published in the People's Republic of China, state authorities renamed it <i>Tintin in China's Tibet</i>. When Casterman and the <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9_Foundation" title="Hergé Foundation">Hergé Foundation</a> protested, the authorities restored the book's original title.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELe_Soir_23_May2001BBC_News_2_June2006_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELe_Soir_23_May2001BBC_News_2_June2006-65"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hergé came to see <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> as his favourite volume in <i>The Adventures of Tintin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Assouline2009189_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Assouline2009189-66"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> He thought it an ode to friendship, composed "under the double sign of tenacity and friendship".<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>h<span>]</span></a></sup> "It's a story of friendship", Hergé said about his book years later, "the way people say, 'It's a love story.'"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009192McCarthy200657_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009192McCarthy200657-69"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>i<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_analysis">Critical analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Critical analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Tintin in Tibet</i> is well received by critics from the comics and literary fields. Farr calls it "exceptional in many respects, standing out among the twenty-three completed <i>Tintin</i> adventures&#160;... an assertion of the incorruptible value of bonds of friendship."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarr2001161-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marc_Lofficier" title="Jean-Marc Lofficier">Jean-Marc Lofficier</a> and Randy Lofficier laud it as "the ultimate <i>Tintin</i> book", reaching "a degree of perfection, both in its story and in its stunning art, that has rarely been equaled, before or since" and "arguably the best book in the series".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200274_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200274-72"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> They detail the story's many emotional moments: Haddock's willingness to sacrifice his life for Tintin's, Tharkey's return, the tearful reunion of Tintin and his starving friend Chang, the reverence paid to Tintin by the Grand Abbot and the monks, and the Yeti's sadness while watching the departure of his only friend. "For a comic book to handle such powerful emotions, convey them to the readers, and make them feel what the characters are feeling is a rare and precious achievement."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200275_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200275-45"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Thompson calls it "a book of overwhelming whiteness and purity",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991171-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> saying that the "intensely personal nature of the story made this Hergé's favourite <i>Tintin</i> adventure", adding that if readers wonder whether "the enormous weight [was] lifted from Hergé's shoulders, [this] can be seen in his next book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Castafiore_Emerald" title="The Castafiore Emerald">The Castafiore Emerald</a></i>, a masterpiece of relaxation."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173.E2.80.93174_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173.E2.80.93174-73"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> As <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> was translated into 32 languages, <a href="/wiki/Donald_S._Lopez,_Jr." title="Donald S. Lopez, Jr.">Donald Lopez</a>, professor of Buddhist and Tibetan studies, calls it the "largest selling book about Tibet."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELopez.2C_Jr.1999212_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELopez.2C_Jr.1999212-74"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Literary critic <a href="/wiki/Jean-Marie_Apostolid%C3%A8s" title="Jean-Marie Apostolidès">Jean-Marie Apostolidès</a>, in a psychoanalytical analysis of <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>, observes that Tintin is more firmly in control of the plot than he was in recent adventures. Apostolidès notes that the character displays worry and emotion not present in previous stories, something he suggested showed Tintin sorting out the problems that he faced in life.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010203_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010203-75"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> In his analysis, he calls Tintin a "<a href="/wiki/Child_abandonment" title="Child abandonment">foundling</a>" and his friend Chang "the lost child" and "Tintin's twin&#160;... the heroes have to struggle to great heights to escape the temporality and pervasive values of [the] universe."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010214_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010214-76"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> He saw the Yeti, who "internalises certain human characteristics", as more complex than Hergé's previous bestial character, Ranko in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Island" title="The Black Island">The Black Island</a></i>:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010215.E2.80.93216_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010215.E2.80.93216-77"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> "The monster loves Chang with a love as unconditional as Tintin's love for his friend."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010220_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEApostolid.C3.A8s2010220-78"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<a href="/wiki/Pierre_Assouline" title="Pierre Assouline">Pierre Assouline</a> calls <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> "a spiritual quest" where the "only conflict is between man and nature."</div>

<p>Likewise, the literary analysis of <a href="/wiki/Tom_McCarthy_(writer)" title="Tom McCarthy (writer)">Tom McCarthy</a> compares Tintin's quest to Hergé's conquest of his own fear and guilt, expounding, "this is the <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai">moira</a> of Hergé's own white mythology, his anaemic destiny: to become <a href="/wiki/Sarrasine" title="Sarrasine">Sarrasine</a> to Tintin's la Zambinella."<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80"><span>[</span>j<span>]</span></a></sup> McCarthy suggested the "icy, white expanses of Hergé's nightmares [may] really have their analogue in his own hero", especially as "Tintin represents an unattainable goal of goodness, cleanness, authenticity."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006160.E2.80.93161_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCarthy2006160.E2.80.93161-81"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Hergé biographer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Assouline" title="Pierre Assouline">Pierre Assouline</a> opines that the work is "a portrait of the artist at a turning point" in his life.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009191_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009191-82"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> He believes that it "stands alone" in <i>The Adventures of Tintin</i> due to its lack of antagonist and few characters, describing it as "a spiritual quest" where the "only conflict is between man and nature&#160;... [Hergé] put the best of himself into <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009191_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAssouline2009191-82"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Referring to its "stripped-bare story and archetypal clarity",<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012274_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012274-83"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Benoît Peeters believes <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> to be one of the two "pivotal" books in the series, alongside <i>The Blue Lotus</i>, and thus deems it poignant that Chang featured in both.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012273_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012273-84"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> He also suggests that Hergé included the benevolent Yeti to "make up for the interminable massacre" of animals in the second Tintin adventure, <i><a href="/wiki/Tintin_in_the_Congo" title="Tintin in the Congo">Tintin in the Congo</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Peeters2012279_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991173Peeters2012279-85"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> and that the sadness the Yeti experienced at the story's end reflected Hergé's feelings about his separation from Germaine.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012281_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012281-86"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Peeters concluded, "Even more than <a href="/wiki/Art_Spiegelman" title="Art Spiegelman">Art Spiegelman</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Maus" title="Maus">Maus</a></i>, <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> is perhaps the most moving book in the history of the comic strip."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012281_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012281-86"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At a ceremony in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> on 1 June 2006, the Dalai Lama bestowed the <a href="/wiki/International_Campaign_for_Tibet" title="International Campaign for Tibet">International Campaign for Tibet</a> (ICT)'s <a href="/wiki/Light_of_Truth_Award" title="Light of Truth Award">Light of Truth Award</a> upon the Hergé Foundation in recognition of <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>, which introduced the region to audiences across the globe.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInt.27l_Campaign_for_Tibet_17_May2006_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInt.27l_Campaign_for_Tibet_17_May2006-87"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> ICT executive director Tsering Jampa said, "For many, Hergé's depiction of Tibet was their introduction to the awe-inspiring landscape and culture of Tibet."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEInt.27l_Campaign_for_Tibet_17_May2006_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEInt.27l_Campaign_for_Tibet_17_May2006-87"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> During the ceremony, copies of <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a> language (<span lang="eo" xml:lang="eo"><i>Tinĉjo en Tibeto</i></span>) were distributed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2_June2006_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2_June2006-88"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> Accepting the award for the foundation, Hergé's widow Fanny Rodwell<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>k<span>]</span></a></sup> said, "We never thought that this story of friendship would have a resonance more than 40 years later".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2_June2006_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_News_2_June2006-88"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tintin_in_Tibet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eight years after Hergé's death, <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> was adapted into an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Tintin (TV series)">The Adventures of Tintin</a></i> (1991–92), a television series by French studio <a href="/wiki/Ellipse_Programm%C3%A9" title="Ellipse Programmé">Ellipse</a> and Canadian animation company <a href="/wiki/Nelvana" title="Nelvana">Nelvana</a>. The episode was directed by Stéphane Bernasconi, with Thierry Wermuth voicing Tintin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200290_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELofficierLofficier200290-91"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> was also a 1992 episode of the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> series <i>The Adventures of Tintin</i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pearce_(British_actor)" title="Richard Pearce (British actor)">Richard Pearce</a> voiced Tintin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBBC_Radio_41993_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBBC_Radio_41993-92"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> The book <a href="/wiki/Tintin_in_Tibet_(video_game)" title="Tintin in Tibet (video game)">became a video game</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">PC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a> in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMobyGames.com1995_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMobyGames.com1995-93"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Tintin_and_I" title="Tintin and I" class="mw-redirect">Tintin and I</a></i> (2003), a documentary by Danish director Anders Høgsbro Østergaard based on Numa Sadoul's 1971 interview with Hergé, includes restored portions of the candid interview that Hergé had heavily edited and rewritten in Sadoul's book.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThompson1991198Peeters2012pp315.E2.80.93317PBS.com2006_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1991198Peeters2012pp315.E2.80.93317PBS.com2006-94"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> With full access to the audio recordings, the filmmaker explored the author's personal battles while creating <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> and how they drove him to create what is now regarded as his most personal adventure.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012pp315.E2.80.93317PBS.com2006_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeeters2012pp315.E2.80.93317PBS.com2006-95"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As the centenary of Hergé's birth approached in 2007, <i>Tintin</i> remained popular.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPollard2007_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPollard2007-96"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Tintin in Tibet</i> was adapted into a theatrical musical, <i>Hergé's Adventures of Tintin</i>, which ran from late 2005 to early 2006 at the <a href="/wiki/Barbican_Arts_Centre" title="Barbican Arts Centre" class="mw-redirect">Barbican Arts Centre</a> in London. The production, directed by Rufus Norris and adapted by Norris and <a href="/wiki/David_Greig_(dramatist)" title="David Greig (dramatist)">David Greig</a>, featured <a href="/wiki/Russell_Tovey" title="Russell Tovey">Russell Tovey</a> as Tintin.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBillington2005YoungVic.org2005Barbican2005_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBillington2005YoungVic.org2005Barbican2005-97"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> The musical was revived at the <a href="/wiki/Playhouse_Theatre" title="Playhouse Theatre">Playhouse Theatre</a> in London's West End before touring in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmurthwaite2007SoniaFriedman.com2007_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmurthwaite2007SoniaFriedman.com2007-98"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2010, the television channel <a href="/wiki/Arte" title="Arte">Arte</a> filmed an episode of its documentary series <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sur_les_traces_de_Tintin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sur les traces de Tintin (page does not exist)">Sur les traces de Tintin</a></i></span> (<i>On the Trail of Tintin</i>) in the Nepalese Himalayas, exploring the inspiration and setting of <i>Tintin in Tibet</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArte2010_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArte2010-99"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> From May to September 2012 the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_Herg%C3%A9" title="Musée Hergé">Musée Hergé</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louvain-la-Neuve" title="Louvain-la-Neuve">Louvain-la-Neuve</a> hosted an exhibition about the book, entitled <i>Into Tibet with Tintin</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMus.C3.A9e_Herg.C3.A92012_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMus.C3.A9e_Herg.C3.A92012-100"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
